Unstuck: Helping Young Adults Reconnect with Purpose and Daily Life

A research-based capstone project on why so many young adults feel stuck and what could actually help.

The Problem

Something's quietly off for a lot of young adults.

A lot of college students and recent grads describe the same feeling: showing up, doing the things, and still feeling completely disconnected from any sense of direction or meaning. It's not burnout, exactly. It's more like... nothing is landing.

This project started from that observation. Research in psychology and behavioral science is pretty clear that purpose, belonging, and daily structure are foundational to wellbeing and when those things slip away, motivation tends to collapse with them.

58%

Report little or no purpose or meaning in daily life

Making Caring Common, 2023

43.3%

Loneliness among 18-34 year olds — highest of any age group

Bruss et al., 2024

51%

Don't know what to do with their lives

Making Caring Common, 2023

Young adults are not lazy. They are under-supported.

Feeling stuck is tied to unmet needs for purpose, belonging, and self-worth. Not personal failure. (Arnett, 2000)

Why It Matters

The stakes are real, even when it doesn't look like a crisis.

Feeling stuck isn't just uncomfortable it compounds. It affects mental health, pulls people away from relationships, makes even small tasks feel impossible, and shapes the habits and identities that people carry forward for years.

The 18-28 window is a genuinely pivotal developmental stretch. The habits, mindsets, and coping strategies formed here tend to stick. That makes it exactly the right moment to offer something useful.

Mental health

Disconnection from purpose correlates strongly with depression, anxiety, and chronic burnout during formative years.

Social belonging

People who feel stuck often withdraw from relationships losing the connection that's essential to resilience.

Daily function

Without structure and direction, small tasks become overwhelming. The cycle reinforces itself.

Long-term trajectory

Early intervention can redirect habits and identities in ways that matter for decades.

Project Prospectus

The initial framing what I set out to investigate, why it matters, and where the thinking started.

Topic

Young adult disconnection and the search for purpose

A problem I've been noticing is the influx of young adults my age who have been feeling lost and completely unsure about where their lives are headed. Initially, I believed this was a problem only I was facing but it turns out that many people in my generation are going through the same things. What makes it worse is that there is no open discussion about this, so there are no open solutions to the many problems we share.

Feeling stuck or behind never feels good. When you let those feelings linger, you begin to lose motivation and question your self-worth. That is not living. My focus is to explore the ways young adults can reconnect with their sense of purpose and hopefully find a path to becoming a better version of themselves.

Main Question

What can I develop to help young adults feel less stuck and more connected to their purpose and daily lives?

This matters to me because there is a misconception that people are just lazy. In reality, people lack the resources to achieve their own piece of contentment. Most of the time, people are just overwhelmed and afraid of making the wrong choices. On top of that, they are burdened with the pressures of social media, social circles, and other external expectations.

That build-up makes it very difficult for "lost" individuals to know where to begin. Understanding this problem helps me think of ways it could be addressed only in the most realistic and supportive way possible.

Why This Matters

This is the time people need the most support, not judgement.

Every day, people walk past thousands of others not knowing what's going on inside their heads almost as if we're supposed to pretend that everything is okay. Truthfully, everyone is struggling with something. For people around my age, we're scrambling to balance school, finances, personal relationships, and constantly worrying about our future plans.

This stage in our lives feels as though we should already know what we're supposed to be doing. That kind of pressure can be very discouraging. I want this project to help people feel and believe that they still have value and potential especially when they don't have everything figured out yet.

Research Connection

Research that challenges norms and centers real experiences

The research I've done relates to this project because it not only helps me understand the developing mind of the human psyche the articles also challenge social norms and embrace themes that would be considered taboo. Studies show that programs focused on connection and belonging reduce symptoms of depression and loneliness in college students (Costello et al., 2022). Arnett (2000) emphasizes that exploring one's identity and sitting with uncertainty are part of a distinct life stage for emerging adults, which explains why so many young adults feel stuck during this transition. Research also shows that having a sense of purpose and mission strengthens mental health and well-being among young adults (Chen et al., 2019).

I'm especially interested in understanding the emotional and social factors behind what causes people to feel stuck, have fears of failure, and make comparisons of themselves to others. External factors like social media and the pressure to succeed are especially important to recognize for this project. These different components will help me identify solutions that are practical and could develop into meaningful routines methods that give young adults the chance to better understand themselves.

Early Ideas for a Solution

A calm, supportive digital resource something you can just open on your phone

I believe this project would become some sort of digital resource. I want this service to feel calm, supportive, and real. There are already similar services that provide a real human connection online, so this will work to an extent. I can imagine it including simple guidance that helps people reflect and have introspective analyses of themselves. The goal is to take small steps and develop change over time.

I can see this project being something people can just open on their phones an easy resource that slowly guides people through understanding who they really are and what they want, then developing small goals to orient themselves into a more meaningful life. This project is aimed to remind young adults that it is okay to not have everything figured out, and that failure is just redirection.

Research & Data

The key findings, themes, and sources that shaped the project's understanding of the problem.

Research approach

Secondary research + reflective observation.

This project drew on a combination of secondary research peer-reviewed articles, published datasets, institutional reports alongside informal primary observations from peer conversations and reflective journaling. The goal was to synthesize what's already known about young adult disconnection and evaluate its implications for a practical solution.

Research spanned four primary themes, each shedding light on a different dimension of the problem.

Psychology of purpose

Why humans need meaning to function and what happens when that need goes unmet.

Mental health trends

Data on rising anxiety, depression, and burnout among 18-26 year olds from campus surveys and national health reports.

Habit & routine

Behavioral science on how daily structure and small habits contribute to emotional stability and forward motion.

Existing interventions

A landscape review of current tools and programs for stuck young adults and an honest read of their gaps.

Key findings

What the research actually revealed.

01

Feeling stuck is a normal part of emerging adulthood, not a personal failure. Arnett (2000) identifies the late teens and twenties as a distinct developmental stage defined by identity exploration and uncertainty. Young adults are no longer following traditional routes. Instead, they experience an "in-between" period that naturally produces instability and a sense of being stuck.

02

Purpose is measurable and it directly affects well-being. Chen et al. (2019) found that young adults with a stronger sense of mission reported higher life satisfaction, stronger emotional processing, and more prosocial behavior. Purpose is not just motivational. It has real, measurable effects on long-term health outcomes.

03

Connection and belonging are central to mental health, not accessories. Costello et al. (2022) found that a peer-based group program significantly reduced depression symptoms and increased school belonging, with the strongest effects among marginalized students. Belonging is both a personal and equity issue.

04

Self-compassion builds resilience and meaning. Chan et al. (2022) found that compassion from others encourages self-compassion, which leads to greater resilience and lower psychological distress among university students. Many young adults fall into cycles of self-blame. Shifting that mindset requires relational support, not just individual effort.

05

Social disconnection is a public health issue, not an individual failure. The Surgeon General's advisory frames loneliness as a societal problem requiring structural solutions, not just personal effort. Young adults should not be blamed for struggling within environments that have failed to support connection.

The Proposed Solution

A human-centered tool designed specifically for young adults who feel stuck built around the experience of being adrift, not the experience of being in crisis.

The Unstuck Framework

A guided, low-barrier digital or print tool that helps young adults identify where they're stuck, reconnect with what matters to them, and take small, meaningful steps forward without overwhelming them in the process.

Who it's for

Designed for the vast, underserved middle.

Not for people in clinical crisis for the much larger group getting by but who've lost their sense of direction, energy, or connection to what they actually care about.

College students

Mid-degree or approaching graduation feeling pressure, uncertainty, and disconnection from why they started.

Recent graduates

Navigating the gap between school and "real life," often without the structure they relied on for years.

Young adults in transition

Changing careers, cities, relationships, or identities and struggling to stay grounded through it.

How it works three phases

The framework is organized around three sequential, self-paced phases. Each takes roughly two to four weeks. Users can return to any phase as needed.

01

Orient name where you are

Short, non-clinical reflection prompts that help users identify the specific texture of their stuck-ness. Is it about direction? Energy? Relationships? Identity? The goal is clarity, not diagnosis moving from a vague feeling to a more specific, workable understanding of what's actually going on.

02

Anchor reconnect with what matters

Values-based and curiosity-based activities that help users reconnect with things they genuinely care about independent of what they "should" care about. Drawn from Self-Determination Theory, this phase rebuilds intrinsic motivation from the inside rather than imposing external goals.

03

Move one small step

A daily micro-habit structure that translates values into small, concrete, sustainable actions. Based on behavioral design principles, this phase is about making forward movement feel achievable building momentum through consistency, not intensity.

Core design principles

Warm, not clinical Brief, not burdensome Self-paced Evidence-based Non-prescriptive Free or low-cost Mobile-friendly Inclusive design

Why it could help

The Unstuck Framework closes several gaps that current resources don't. It meets users at the level of mild-to-moderate disconnection rather than crisis. It uses language and tone calibrated for this specific population. It's grounded in mechanisms purpose, self-determination, habit formation that research has shown actually work.

Most importantly, it treats young adults as capable people who know themselves and just need a structured, supportive process to hear themselves more clearly.

Project Summary

What the research and project mean, taken together and a preliminary answer to the central question.

What was learned

The full picture.

This project began with a question rooted in personal observation and social concern: why do so many young adults feel stuck, and what could actually help? Over the course of the research and design process, that question became both more complex and more answerable.

What became clear is that feeling stuck isn't a character flaw or a phase to wait out. It's a predictable outcome of unmet psychological needs for autonomy, connection, and competence in a life stage that's genuinely difficult and dramatically underserved. Understanding this reframes the problem from "what's wrong with these young adults" to "what conditions would actually help them?"

"Young adults don't need to be fixed. They need to be heard, supported, and given a small enough entry point to start moving again."

Preliminary answer to the project question

What helps young adults reconnect with purpose and re-engage with daily life when they feel stuck? Based on the research, the most meaningful answer points to three things working together: clarity about where they are (naming the specific nature of their disconnection), reconnection with what genuinely matters (rebuilding intrinsic motivation), and small, consistent actions that generate a sense of agency and forward movement.

No single app, workshop, or book does all three well for this population. So I built one. The Unstuck app is a working prototype of the framework, something you can open, use, and experience right now, not just read about.

Final takeaways

one

The problem is real and underaddressed

Mental health resources cluster at the crisis end. The vast middle ground people who aren't in crisis but aren't thriving either is largely neglected.

two

Purpose is a psychological necessity

When it's absent, everything else in daily life becomes harder to sustain. This isn't abstract it's functional.

three

Small steps matter more than they seem

Tiny, sustainable actions compound into meaningful identity and habit change. Momentum beats intensity.

four

Design and tone are functional, not cosmetic

How a tool speaks to its users determines whether they engage at all. Warmth is a feature, not decoration.

five

This population deserves investment

Young adults 18-28 are at a pivotal developmental juncture. The support structures available haven't kept pace with the complexity of that moment.

six

This is a beginning, not an ending

The Unstuck Framework needs further testing, refinement, and input from the people it's meant to serve. This project is a starting point.

Final Reflection

A personal account of the capstone experience: what it taught me, what challenged me, and what I carry forward.

The Program

A shift in mental framework.

I believe that by having taken part of the BPS program, it has shifted my mental framework for the better. I came into the program not knowing what it was or how the teaching style was. Compared to traditional medical or business courses, it would be the same ones, just harder every year. In a way, the program offered that. Each course, upon completing one, the next challenges your ability to not only think analytically but by using emotional intelligence to spearhead decisions.

Leadership, naturally

I have always seen myself as the one who plays the "leader" role. In my family, I am the first born and older brother. In school I am the first to volunteer. In social groups I make the plans. The project management course (IPS 408) was one I was surely not short of being familiar with. I had completed a project management internship the summer prior to taking the course, and there were not only familiar problem-solving methods I had seen but, most importantly, several transferrable skills I took and applied within this capstone project.

Problem solving people, not projects

When it comes to problem solving, of course a plan is required, but it is nearly impossible to have one without fully understanding the problem at hand. In my case, it is a little more complex. Rather than planning for a business or a literal project venture, I am working towards solving human behavior. In a way, that is what all things are. But when it comes to feelings of anxiety and uneasiness, having the proper toolset to get out of that rut is very important. That is why in my research, I found it imperative for individuals who are currently facing this to develop routines and thoroughly analyze the root causes of what makes them feel that way. In a way, I am helping individuals become project managers of their own selves.

"I am helping individuals become project managers of their own selves."

Rewriting the path

In the time it has taken me to complete this degree, I have learned that the pathways set out for me do not have to be the ones I have to follow. Prior to pursuing this degree, I was on a business track, and before that a medical track. Both of which I was expected to pursue and finish. Maybe one day, just not now. This degree has helped me further analyze career paths that best fit my interests and given me the ability to gauge what I am capable of doing.

Career wise, this degree empowers individuals to be action takers in starting a business or being a prominent figure in one. My professional goals absolutely did change. I was not sure where I wanted to end up. Which is also why this project is particularly important to me, because I was one of those people who woke up stressed every day, unsure of what actions I was going to take that would contribute to my "career goals." Now, I have learned to play on my strengths rather than following the preconceived plan others may have set out for me.
